## Why bother with the same name everywhere

Every platform you join asks for a username. If you pick something different each time, you're making it harder for people to find you. Someone who follows you on [Instagram](/check/instagram) shouldn't have to guess what you go by on [TikTok](/check/tiktok) or [Twitter](/check/twitter).

This isn't a vanity thing. When someone sees the same handle on a podcast bio, a [GitHub](/check/github) repo, and a [YouTube](/check/youtube) channel, it clicks. Same person. Real brand.

The practical reasons: people search for you by username on new platforms, and they should find you on the first try. Mismatched handles look scattered if you're freelancing or running a business. And if you leave your preferred name unclaimed somewhere, someone else can grab it.

## Audit what you already have

Before picking a new username, figure out where you stand. Open a spreadsheet or a notes app and list every platform you have an account on. Write down the username for each one.

You'll probably find a mess. Maybe you're `jakedesigns` on Instagram but `jake_designs_` on Twitter and `jaked2024` on TikTok. That's normal. Most people signed up for platforms years apart and picked whatever was available at the time.

Once you see everything laid out, patterns emerge. Maybe one username is already consistent across three or four platforms. That's a good starting point.

Check for accounts you forgot about too. Old Tumblr profiles, abandoned Reddit accounts, that Behance page from 2019. These are either assets (you already own the name) or liabilities (they show up in search results with outdated info).

## What makes a good universal username

Not every username works everywhere. Platform rules vary. Some allow periods, others don't. Some have 30-character limits, others cap at 15. If you want the same username on every platform, you need to pick one that fits the strictest rules.

Here's what to aim for:

### Keep it short

Under 15 characters. This fits within the limits of nearly every platform. Shorter is better for another reason too: people need to type it, spell it, and remember it. `alivera` beats `alexandravera_official`.

If you want to know exactly how long each platform allows, we've put together a breakdown of [character limits on every platform](/blog/username-character-limits).

### Stick to safe characters

Letters and numbers. That's the universal safe set. Underscores work on most platforms but not all. Periods work on some. Hyphens are hit or miss.

The safest bet is lowercase letters only. No spaces, no special characters, no accented letters. If your username is `oakstudio`, it'll work everywhere. If it's `oak.studio`, you'll run into problems on platforms that don't allow periods.

### Make it easy to say out loud

This gets overlooked. You'll say your username in conversations, on podcasts, in videos. If someone asks "where can I find you?" and you have to spell out a complicated handle letter by letter, you've already lost them.

Test it: say your username out loud to someone and ask them to type it. If they get it right on the first try, you're good. If they ask "is that with an underscore or a hyphen?" or "is there a number at the end?", keep looking.

### Avoid numbers (usually)

Numbers make usernames harder to remember and harder to communicate verbally. "Is that the number 5 or the word five?" Nobody wants to have that conversation.

The exception is when the number is part of your actual brand. If you're a design studio called Studio44, then `studio44` is fine. But if you're adding `99` to the end because `jakemiller` was taken, that's a compromise you'll regret.

### Prioritize the platforms that matter

Not every platform carries equal weight for your situation. A photographer cares more about Instagram and Behance than GitHub. A developer cares more about GitHub and Stack Overflow than Pinterest.

Make a tier list:

- **Tier 1:** Platforms you actively use and post on
- **Tier 2:** Platforms you might use in the next year or two
- **Tier 3:** Major platforms you don't use but want to protect your name on

Your username needs to be available on every Tier 1 platform. Ideally Tier 2 as well.

### Register defensively

Once you've found a username that's available everywhere (or nearly everywhere), claim it. Even on platforms you don't plan to use right now.

This takes 20 minutes and saves you from a real headache later. If you decide to start a YouTube channel in six months and someone else has already taken your handle, you're stuck.

You don't need to post anything. Just create the account, set a profile picture, add a one-line bio, and leave it. The account exists. The name is yours.

A note: some platforms recycle inactive usernames after long periods. Check back occasionally to make sure your reserved accounts are still active.

## When your preferred username is taken

You searched, and the results are mixed. Your ideal username is available on 8 out of 10 platforms you care about but taken on [Instagram](/check/instagram) and [Twitter](/check/twitter). Now what?

You have a few options.

### Try variations

Small modifications can work if they're consistent. If `oakstudio` is taken, try:

- `oakstudiohq` (adding "hq" for headquarters)
- `theoakstudio` (adding "the")
- `getoakstudio` (adding "get", common for apps and brands)

The key is to use the same variation everywhere, even on platforms where the base name is available. Consistency matters more than getting the shortest possible handle.

For more strategies on this, read our guide on [what to do when your username is taken](/blog/username-already-taken).

### Check if the account is actually active

Sometimes a taken username belongs to a dead account. No posts, no profile picture, no activity in years. Some platforms will release these usernames if you contact support. Twitter and Instagram both have processes for this, though success isn't guaranteed.

It's worth the email. The worst they can say is no.

### Understand username vs. display name

On most platforms, your display name and your username are different things. Your username is the unique handle (the `@` name). Your display name is what appears in bold on your profile.

So if `oliviapark` is taken as a username but you manage to get `oliviaparkco`, you can still set your display name to "Olivia Park" on every platform. The display name provides recognition. The username provides the link.

## Examples of good cross-platform usernames

Here are some patterns that work well as universal usernames. These are fictional examples to illustrate the principles.

**`maplecreative`** - 14 characters, all letters, easy to say, clear meaning. Works for a design brand or creative studio.

**`tomkra`** - 6 characters, short, all letters. A first name plus truncated last name. Simple and memorable.

**`buildwithjay`** - 12 characters, descriptive, all letters. Works for a developer or maker who goes by Jay.

**`thecozyshop`** - 11 characters, brandable, all letters. Good for a small business. Adding "the" is a common trick to get an available name.

**`lunarunlabs`** - 10 characters, unique, all letters. Doesn't mean anything obvious, which means it's probably available everywhere.

Notice what these have in common: all lowercase, no special characters, under 15 characters, easy to pronounce. None of them end in random numbers or use underscores as separators.

## Platform-specific things to watch for

Even with a universal username, some platforms have quirks worth knowing about.

[Instagram](/check/instagram) allows periods and underscores but won't let you use them consecutively. Minimum 1 character, maximum 30.

[Twitter](/check/twitter) allows underscores but not periods. Maximum 15 characters. This is often the bottleneck for username length.

[TikTok](/check/tiktok) allows underscores and periods. Maximum 24 characters. Minimum 2.

[GitHub](/check/github) allows hyphens but not underscores at the start or end. Maximum 39 characters.

[YouTube](/check/youtube) handles are newer and require the `@` prefix. Maximum 30 characters.
