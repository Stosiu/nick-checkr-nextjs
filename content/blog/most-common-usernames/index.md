NordPass analyzed millions of leaked database entries to find the most common usernames on the internet. The results are boring in the most predictable way: people type their first name into the username field and hit enter.

The top five? David (470,646 hits), Alex (451,546), Maria (438,485), Anna (387,660), and Marco (352,629). The actual #1 was "ยศกร" -- a Thai word meaning "title" -- with 875,562 hits, which says more about the dataset's geographic makeup than anything else. But the pattern is clear. The internet's most popular usernames aren't clever. They're just names.

## Why this is everyone's first instinct

Nobody's first account was a branding exercise. You signed up for Neopets or AIM or whatever existed when you were twelve, typed your name, and moved on. That instinct never really went away. Most people still try their real name first, even when creating their fifth or sixth account across platforms.

The problem is math. [Instagram](/check/instagram) crossed 3 billion monthly active users in late 2025. [Twitter](/check/twitter) has been around since 2006. Even if only a fraction of accounts on each platform are active, the namespace is wrecked. There are roughly 26^6 possible six-letter lowercase combinations -- about 308 million. Instagram alone has ten times that many users. Every common English word, every common first name, every obvious compound ("coolcat," "darknight," "musiclover") was taken years ago.

This is why you keep hitting the "username already taken" screen. It's just math.

## The geography of username scarcity

Not all names hit the wall at the same time. English-speaking names saturated first because American platforms dominated early adoption. If your name is James, Sarah, Mike, or Chris, your options on any platform older than 2015 are essentially zero without adding numbers or underscores.

Spanish and Portuguese names followed. Maria, Carlos, Ana, and Pedro are taken across most major platforms. Arabic names are catching up fast. Muhammad (and its many transliterations -- Mohammed, Mohamed, Mehmed, Mohammad) is the most common first name in the world, carried by an estimated 150 million people. When that many people want the same handle, even creative spelling variations get claimed quickly.

There's a less obvious factor: romanization. Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Hindi names get transliterated into Latin characters for most platform usernames, which compresses millions of distinct names into a much smaller set of available spellings. "Wei" in Chinese could be dozens of different characters with different meanings, but on [Instagram](/check/instagram) they all compete for the same five-letter handle.

Some platforms still have inventory for non-English names, but the window is closing. [Bluesky](/check/bluesky) grew from 10 million to 40 million users in a single year. [Threads](/check/threads) is tied to Instagram accounts for now but may eventually separate usernames. New platforms always start with plenty of names available. Give it two or three years and the obvious ones are gone.

## Not all platforms are equally saturated

A username being taken on [Twitter](/check/twitter) doesn't mean it's taken on [Steam](/check/steam). Platform age, user base size, and naming conventions all affect scarcity differently.

Twitter and Instagram are the most saturated. Both have been around for well over a decade, both have massive user bases, and both allow very short usernames. On Twitter, even most four-letter combinations are claimed. On Instagram, three-letter handles sell for hundreds of dollars on secondary markets.

Gaming platforms have a different scarcity pattern. [Steam](/check/steam), [Xbox](/check/xbox), and [PSN](/check/playstation-network) usernames tend toward gamertags rather than real names, so "NightViper" might be taken but "David" could be available on some gaming services. [Discord](/check/discord) reset its entire namespace in 2023 when it dropped the discriminator system (#1234), which briefly opened up handles that had been unavailable for years. If you missed that window, read our [Discord username guide](/blog/discord-username-guide) for what the new system looks like.

Professional platforms work differently too. [LinkedIn](/check/linkedin) uses firstname-lastname URL slugs, so common name collisions are handled with numbers (/in/john-smith-4827/) rather than forcing users to get creative. [GitHub](/check/github) skews toward developer handles and pseudonyms, which creates its own scarcity patterns around tech-adjacent words.

The point is that "my username is taken everywhere" is rarely literally true. It's taken on the three platforms you checked. A tool that checks [hundreds of platforms at once](https://nickcheckr.com) usually turns up openings you wouldn't have thought to look for.

## What the data actually tells you

The NordPass dataset and similar analyses confirm something most people already suspect but don't quantify: if your name is common, your username problem isn't a you problem. It's a hundreds-of-thousands-of-other-people problem.

Some numbers to put it in perspective. The top 200 most common usernames account for millions of database entries. The top 10 first names in the United States (James, Robert, John, Michael, David, William, Richard, Joseph, Thomas, Charles for men; Mary, Patricia, Jennifer, Linda, Barbara, Elizabeth, Susan, Jessica, Sarah, Karen for women) have been claimed on every major platform since the early 2010s. Globally, the concentration is even steeper -- a handful of names in each language group absorb a disproportionate share of the namespace.

The frustration goes beyond the signup screen. People with common names end up stuck with usernames that include birth years (sarah1994), locations (mikeNYC), or random numbers (david_382). None of those are great for branding. Worse, the handle you reluctantly accept on one platform may not even be available on the next one, which makes [keeping the same username across platforms](/blog/same-username-everywhere) nearly impossible.
