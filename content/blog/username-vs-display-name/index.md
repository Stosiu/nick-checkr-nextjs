## The core distinction

Every platform gives you two identities: a **username** and a **display name**. They look similar on screen but work differently.

Your **username** is your unique identifier. It shows up in your profile URL, gets used when someone @mentions you, and is what you type to log in. No two people on the same platform can have the same username. That's the whole point.

Your **display name** is the label people see on your profile, in comment threads, and next to your posts. It doesn't need to be unique. Five thousand people on Instagram can all have "John Smith" as their display name. Nobody cares because the display name isn't used to find or identify your account in any technical sense.

The practical difference: if you want to tag someone in a post, you use their username. If you want to know what someone calls themselves, you look at their display name.

This matters when you're [trying to use the same username everywhere](/blog/same-username-everywhere). Here's how each major platform handles it.

## Platform-by-platform breakdown

### Instagram

On [Instagram](/check/instagram), your **username** is your @handle. It's what appears in your profile URL (`instagram.com/yourname`) and what people type after the @ symbol to tag you. It must be unique, can only contain letters, numbers, periods, and underscores, and has a 30-character limit.

Your **Name** field (the display name) appears in bold at the top of your profile. It can contain spaces, special characters, and can be identical to someone else's. You can change it whenever you want without any waiting period.

This is one of the most common sources of confusion — people search "instagram name vs username" because the app labels them differently in settings. The username is the one that matters for discoverability. When someone searches for you, Instagram weighs your username more heavily than your display name.

### TikTok

[TikTok](/check/tiktok) follows a similar pattern. Your username appears in your profile URL (`tiktok.com/@yourname`) and in @mentions. It must be unique and is restricted to letters, numbers, underscores, and periods.

Your display name — what TikTok just calls your "name" — shows on your profile above your username. It can include spaces, special characters, and doesn't need to be unique. Most creators use their display name for branding (their real name or brand name) while keeping a clean, memorable username for the URL.

### Twitter / X

[Twitter](/check/twitter) has one of the stricter setups. Your @handle (username) is capped at 15 characters — shorter than most platforms. It appears in your URL, in @mentions, and is the primary way people find you. Only letters, numbers, and underscores are allowed.

Your display name can be up to 50 characters and accepts almost anything — spaces, special characters, even text that looks nothing like a name. Some users change their display name constantly for jokes or announcements. The @handle stays fixed (unless you deliberately change it in settings, which is possible but risky since someone else can grab your old one).

### YouTube

[YouTube](/check/youtube) introduced the @handle system relatively recently. Before that, channels only had names. Now you get a unique @handle that appears in your channel URL (`youtube.com/@yourhandle`) and can be used to mention you in comments.

Your channel name is the display name. It can be anything, doesn't need to be unique, and you can change it. The @handle is the identifier — the channel name is the brand.

### Reddit

[Reddit](/check/reddit) is one of the strictest platforms when it comes to usernames. Your `u/username` is **permanent**. You cannot change it. Ever. If you pick a bad one, your only option is to create a new account.

Reddit later added an optional display name that appears on your profile. But the username is what shows up in every comment and post you make across the site. This makes choosing the right Reddit username especially important — check the [username character limits on every platform](/blog/username-character-limits) before you commit.

### Discord

Discord overhauled its username system in 2023, moving away from the old `Name#1234` discriminator format. Now, everyone gets a unique lowercase username (letters, numbers, underscores, periods). This functions like a username on any other platform — unique, used for adding friends, appears in your profile.

On top of that, you have a **display name** that's global, plus **server nicknames** that are specific to individual servers. So on Discord, you technically have three layers of identity: username, display name, and per-server nickname. The username is the only one that must be unique.

### Twitch

[Twitch](/check/twitch) keeps it simple but with an unusual twist. Your username is your login, your channel URL (`twitch.tv/yourname`), and how people @mention you in chat.

Your "display name" on Twitch is just your username with different capitalization. You can't set an entirely different display name — you can only change the casing. So if your username is `coolstreamer`, your display name could be `CoolStreamer` or `COOLSTREAMER`, but not something else entirely.

### Steam

[Steam](/check/steam) is at the other extreme. Your display name (what Steam calls your "profile name") can be changed freely, at any time, to literally anything. It doesn't need to be unique. You can change it five times in one day if you want.

Your actual identifier is either your Steam ID (a number) or your custom URL (`steamcommunity.com/id/yourname`). The custom URL functions as a username — it's unique and appears in your profile link — but most Steam users interact through display names, not profile URLs.

### GitHub

On [GitHub](/check/github), your username is embedded in everything. Every repository you own lives at `github.com/yourname/repo`. Your profile, your contributions, your gists — all tied to the username. Changing it is possible but breaks every existing link to your repositories unless GitHub sets up redirects.

Your profile name (display name) is what shows on your profile page and in commit author lines. It's separate from the username and can be changed freely.

### Spotify

[Spotify](/check/spotify) is an oddball. Your username is auto-generated — it's a random string of numbers and letters that you can't change. You never chose it and you probably don't know what it is. It's used internally and for your profile URL, but nobody actually uses it to find you.

Your display name is what appears on your profile and in shared playlists. It's the only identity that matters in practice. Spotify is one of the few platforms where the display name is more important than the username.

## Why this matters

Most platforms let you change your display name whenever you want. Your username is a different story. Reddit won't let you change it at all. GitHub sets up redirects but old links can still break. Twitter lets you change it, but someone else can immediately claim the old one. Pick your username carefully because you might be stuck with it.

On Google, your username appears in the URL of your profile, and URLs influence search rankings. If your username is your brand name, that helps. If it's `xX_darkn1ght_Xx`, it doesn't.

## Which one should you optimize for?

Both, but prioritize the username.

Your username is the foundation. It's in every URL, every @mention, and often can't be changed. If a username you want is [already taken on some platforms](/blog/username-already-taken), it's worth finding an alternative that's available everywhere rather than using different usernames on different sites.

Your display name is the surface layer. It's what people see, but it's flexible. You can update it as your brand evolves, add context (like a job title or location), or match it to a campaign. It's low-stakes because you can always change it back.

The one exception is Spotify, where the username is irrelevant and the display name is all that matters. But for every other major platform, get the username right first.
