YouTube has three different things that look like usernames: your channel name, your handle, and your custom URL. If that sounds confusing, it's because it is. YouTube has changed this system multiple times over the years, and the result is a layered mess that trips people up constantly.

Here's how it actually works in 2026, and how to claim the URL you want.

## Handles, custom URLs, and channel names

These three things serve different purposes, and mixing them up leads to the wrong settings page and wasted time.

Your **channel name** is your display name. It shows up on your channel page, next to your comments, and in search results. It doesn't need to be unique. You can change it whenever you want. If you're unclear on the difference between usernames and display names across platforms, that distinction is [explained in detail here](/blog/username-vs-display-name).

Your **handle** is the @ identifier. It looks like `@yourname` and shows up in your channel URL (`youtube.com/@yourname`), in mentions, and in Shorts. Handles were introduced in late 2022, and YouTube has been pushing them as the primary identity system ever since. Handles must be unique.

Your **custom URL** used to be a separate thing. YouTube previously let eligible channels claim a vanity URL like `youtube.com/c/yourname`. That system is gone. Handles replaced it. If you had an old custom URL, YouTube migrated it to a handle automatically. Some channels still have legacy URLs that redirect, but you can't create new `/c/` URLs anymore.

So when people search for "YouTube custom URL" in 2026, what they actually want is a handle. That's what we'll set up.

## Eligibility requirements

Not every channel can claim a handle right away. YouTube requires:

- At least 100 subscribers
- An account that's at least 30 days old
- A profile picture uploaded
- A banner image uploaded

If you just created your channel yesterday, you'll need to wait. The subscriber requirement is the real bottleneck for new creators. Until you hit 100, YouTube assigns you a default handle based on your channel name with a string of random characters appended.

You can check if your channel qualifies by going to YouTube Studio and looking under Customization. If the handle field is editable, you're eligible.

## How to set your handle

The process takes about two minutes once you're eligible.

1. Go to [YouTube Studio](https://studio.youtube.com)
2. Click **Customization** in the left sidebar
3. Select the **Basic info** tab
4. Find the **Handle** field
5. Type the handle you want (without the @)
6. YouTube will immediately tell you if it's available
7. Click **Publish** in the top right

That's it. Your channel URL becomes `youtube.com/@yourhandle` immediately. Old links to your channel (the `/channel/UC...` format) still work and redirect.

One thing to note: YouTube limits how often you can change your handle. You can change it, but there's a cooldown period, and YouTube warns that frequent changes may result in losing the ability to change it at all. Pick something you'll stick with.

## What if the handle you want is taken

This is the frustrating part. Short, clean handles on [YouTube](/check/youtube) were claimed years ago, either by active creators or by people who created accounts and never posted.

Your options:

Start by checking if the handle is actually in use. Search for it on YouTube. If the channel that owns it has zero videos and no activity, you're out of luck for now. YouTube doesn't have a formal inactivity reclamation process for handles, though they've hinted at eventually freeing up unused ones.

Try variations. YouTube handles allow letters, numbers, underscores, hyphens, and periods. That gives you more flexibility than platforms like Twitter, which only allows underscores. Something like `@jake.designs` or `@jake-designs` might be available when `@jakedesigns` isn't. Check our [guide to character limits across platforms](/blog/username-character-limits) to see how YouTube compares.

If someone is squatting on a handle that matches your registered trademark, you can file a trademark claim through YouTube's complaint process. This only works if you have an actual trademark registration. Being annoyed that someone took your preferred name doesn't qualify.

If your name is common, adding a niche descriptor can work well. `@sarahcooks` or `@jakecodes` tells viewers something about your content and is more likely to be available than just your first name. For more strategies, read about [what to do when your username is already taken](/blog/username-already-taken).

## Handle rules and restrictions

YouTube handles follow specific rules:

- 3 to 30 characters long
- Must contain at least one letter
- Can include letters, numbers, underscores, hyphens, and periods
- Cannot be only numbers
- Cannot contain spaces
- Are not case-sensitive (`@JakeDesigns` and `@jakedesigns` point to the same channel)
- Cannot impersonate another person or organization

The 3-30 character range is generous compared to most platforms. Twitter caps you at 15 characters. Snapchat won't go above 15 either. YouTube gives you room to work with, but shorter handles are still better. Nobody wants to type out a 25-character handle to tag you in a comment.

## Tips for choosing a good handle

If you're `@oakstudio` on [Instagram](/check/instagram) and [TikTok](/check/tiktok), try to get the same handle on YouTube. Consistent handles make you easier to find and look more professional. Having different names on every platform is a common mistake that's worth [reading about separately](/blog/same-username-everywhere).

Keep it under 15 characters if you can. This ensures compatibility with Twitter and other restrictive platforms. It's also easier to say out loud in a video when you tell viewers where to find you.

Skip the numbers. Adding birth years or random digits to the end of a handle looks rushed. `@sarah2003` says "my first choice was taken" louder than it says anything about your content. If you must use a number, make it meaningful to your brand.

You'll be saying your handle on camera, in podcasts, in conversations. "Find me at oak studio" rolls off the tongue. "Find me at oak underscore studio underscore official" does not.

Your handle also doesn't need to match your channel name verbatim. Your channel name (display name) can be "The Official Oak Studio Design Agency." Your handle should be `@oakstudio`.

## Common issues

"This handle isn't available" but the channel doesn't exist? YouTube reserves some handles for trademark holders, partner channels, and other reasons. A handle can be unavailable even if nobody visibly owns it.

If you changed from one handle to another, the old `@oldname` URL is released after a short holding period. It won't redirect forever. Someone else could eventually claim your old handle.

If your handle shows random characters, your channel hasn't met the eligibility requirements yet. Hit 100 subscribers and the 30-day age mark, and you'll be able to set a real handle.

Can't find the handle setting? Make sure you're in YouTube Studio (studio.youtube.com), not the main YouTube site. It's under Customization > Basic info, not under Settings.
