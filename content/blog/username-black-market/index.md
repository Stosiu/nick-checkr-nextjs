In 2023, Twitter -- freshly rebranded to X -- seized the @x handle from Gene X. Hwang, a corporate photographer who'd held it since 2007. No warning, no compensation. The company just took it. Hwang got a form letter and a new handle he didn't ask for.

One character. One platform. Valuable enough for a billion-dollar company to just take it from someone. And nobody was particularly surprised, which tells you a lot about where we are with usernames.

## What makes a username "OG"

"OG" in this context means original -- early, short, clean. The fewer characters, the better. A single letter on [Instagram](/check/instagram) (@a, @b, @x) sits at the top of the hierarchy. Two letters are next. Then single common English words (@wolf, @rain, @cloud). Then common first names without numbers, underscores, or any other decoration.

The logic is the same as domain names in the 1990s. There are only 26 possible single-letter handles. Only 676 two-letter combinations. The supply is fixed and tiny. As platforms grew from thousands of users to billions, these handles became scarce in a way that can't be reversed. Nobody's making more of them.

What gives an OG handle its cachet isn't the word itself. It's what the handle implies: you were there first. On a platform with 3 billion users, holding @david or @kate without a single number or underscore is proof of tenure. It's the internet equivalent of a low-digit license plate -- functionally identical to any other, but unmistakably rare.

## From bragging rights to real money

For most of the internet's history, having a short handle was just a personal flex. You'd mention it in passing. People would ask how you got it. The answer was always the same: I signed up early.

The shift happened around 2012-2015, when Instagram and Twitter became central to personal branding. Influencers, musicians, and small businesses realized that a clean handle mattered for discoverability, credibility, and just looking legitimate. A brand operating as @coolshoes is more trustworthy than @coolshoes_official_2019. The demand was there, and supply was fixed.

A market formed. Informal at first -- DMs, forum posts, handshake deals. Then more structured. Marketplaces like SWAPD, PlayerUp, and EpicNPC built escrow systems and price histories around handle trading. A three-letter Instagram handle runs about $650. Four-letter [Twitter](/check/twitter) handles sell for $800 to $1,300, with recognizable English words commanding premiums -- "clarifies" sold for $1,300, "rampart" for $1,100. Single-letter handles on Instagram are reportedly worth up to $50,000.

Telegram went even further. When Telegram introduced tradable usernames on the TON blockchain, it turned handles into something closer to financial instruments. You can see floor prices, trading volume, historical data. A Telegram username like @crypto or @shop has real, visible market value. The speculation mirrors early domain name investing -- buy something short and generic, wait for someone to need it badly enough to pay.

None of this is officially sanctioned by most platforms. [Instagram's](/check/instagram) terms of service prohibit selling accounts. [Twitter's](/check/twitter) did too. But enforcement has always been half-hearted, and the market kept growing because the underlying problem never changed. People want short handles. There aren't enough of them. No platform offers a legitimate way to transfer one.

## Where it turned criminal

The OGUsers forum, launched in 2017, was where the handle-trading community concentrated. At its peak it had over 50,000 members buying and selling social media handles, gaming accounts, and the methods to acquire them. It was also where things got dark.

The most common theft technique was SIM swapping. Attackers would convince a mobile carrier to transfer a target's phone number to a new SIM card, then use that number to reset passwords and take over accounts with valuable handles. Joel Ortiz, a 20-year-old from Boston, was the first person arrested specifically for SIM swapping. He'd hijacked at least 40 people's phone numbers, stealing over $5 million in cryptocurrency along the way. The handles were often the initial target -- the crypto theft was a bonus.

The 2020 Twitter hack -- the one where Barack Obama, Elon Musk, and Apple's official accounts all tweeted a Bitcoin scam simultaneously -- had roots in this same community. Graham Ivan Clark, the 17-year-old regarded as the attack's mastermind, was a former OGUsers member. Two other participants, Mason Sheppard and Nima Fazeli, were brokers who sold [Twitter](/check/twitter) handles on the forum.

OGUsers itself was hacked multiple times, leaking its members' data to the public. A community built around stealing accounts kept getting its own accounts stolen. Law enforcement used the leaked databases to build cases. Instagram sent cease-and-desist letters to prominent handle traders. The FBI made multiple arrests.

The forum eventually went offline, but the market didn't disappear. It spread across [Discord](/check/discord) servers, Telegram groups, and newer forums. The demand for short handles didn't go away just because the most visible marketplace got burned.

## Platforms can't decide what to do

The platform response to handle trading has been contradictory for years.

Twitter announced in May 2023 that it would purge inactive accounts, potentially freeing up 1.5 billion usernames held by dormant profiles. The internet briefly got excited. Then Twitter delayed the purge after users pointed out that it would erase the accounts of deceased people -- a memorialization problem nobody had planned for.

Then X did something unexpected: it entered the market itself. Code discovered in the X app revealed a "Handle Marketplace" where Verified Organizations (who already pay $1,000/month) can bid on dormant handles. Starting price: $10,000. Premium handles could go for over $500,000. The platform that spent years telling users they couldn't sell handles was now selling them directly.

Telegram embraced trading from the start through blockchain integration. [Discord](/check/discord) sidestepped the problem by [resetting its entire namespace](/blog/discord-username-guide) in 2023, dropping the discriminator system and forcing everyone to claim new unique handles. That temporarily created a land rush, but it also meant Discord's secondary market started from scratch.

Instagram and most other platforms still officially prohibit handle sales while doing very little to stop them. As long as that gap exists, the market will too.

## What this has to do with you

You're probably not buying a $10,000 handle. But the forces that created this market are the same ones that make your preferred username unavailable on [Instagram](/check/instagram), [TikTok](/check/tiktok), or [Twitter](/check/twitter).

Username scarcity isn't abstract. It's the reason you're "sarah_designs_" instead of "sarah." Brands end up with awkward handles that don't match their domain name. [Having the same username across all platforms](/blog/same-username-everywhere) is difficult because every platform has different names locked up by different people for different reasons.

The practical lesson: claim your preferred handle on platforms early, even ones you don't plan to use yet. When a new platform launches, the namespace is wide open. By the time it has 100 million users, the [good names are gone](/blog/username-already-taken). The difference between a clean handle and one with numbers tacked on is almost always timing.
