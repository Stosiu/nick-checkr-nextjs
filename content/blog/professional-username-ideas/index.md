## The default formula still wins

Your professional username should be your name. That's it. `firstnamelastname`, `firstname.lastname`, or `firstinitiallastname`. On [LinkedIn](/check/linkedin), your custom URL slug is literally designed for this: `linkedin.com/in/sarahjohnson`. On [GitHub](/check/github), the cleanest profiles are the ones that just use a name: `github.com/alexgarcia`.

This isn't about creativity. Professional platforms are places where people look you up before a meeting, after reading your code, or when deciding whether to hire you. They need to find you quickly, and they'll search your real name. A username that matches it removes all friction.

The formula, ranked by preference:

- `sarahjohnson` or `alexgarcia` (full name, no separator)
- `sarah.johnson` or `alex-garcia` (with separator, where allowed)
- `sjohnson` or `agarcia` (first initial + last name)

If your name is short and distinctive, you might get the exact match. If your name is Sarah Johnson, Alex Garcia, or Mike Smith, you won't. That's where things get interesting.

## When your name is already taken

Common names collide constantly on professional platforms. There are roughly 45,000 people named "David Johnson" in the US alone. Every short variation of that name was claimed on [LinkedIn](/check/linkedin) a decade ago. If that's you, there's no point getting frustrated. You just need a strategy.

### Add a middle initial

The simplest fix. `jdsmith`, `sarahkjones`, `michaeltlee`. It's unambiguous, professional, and it's what most people do on business cards and email signatures anyway. If you already use your middle initial professionally, this is the obvious choice.

### Add an industry keyword

This pulls double duty: it solves the collision and signals what you do. Good options for different fields:

- `alexcodes`, `sarahcodes`, `mikecodes`
- `sarahdesigns`, `alexdesigns`, `mikedesigns`
- `jenteaches`, `tomteaches`
- `mikebuilds`, `alexbuilds`, `sarahbuilds`

The verb format (`nameverb`) reads naturally and stays compact. It works particularly well on GitHub, where `alexcodes` immediately tells visitors what kind of account this is. The tradeoff: if you change careers, the keyword ages badly. For most people in established careers, the clarity is worth it.

### Add a location

Works best if you're tied to a specific city professionally. `alexnyc`, `sarahldn`, `mikesf`, `jenchi`, `tombln`. This is common among consultants and freelancers who serve a local market. It's less useful if you're remote-first or planning to relocate, since you'll be stuck with a city that no longer applies.

### Use a name abbreviation

Sometimes the answer is just shortening your name in a way that still reads as you. `benj` instead of `benjamin`, `christo` instead of `christopher`, `alexg` instead of `alex garcia`, `danw` instead of `daniel williams`. These feel natural and are often available when the full version isn't.

## By profession

Different fields have different conventions. What works on GitHub would look odd on LinkedIn, and what works for a designer wouldn't suit a financial consultant. Here's what tends to work in each space.

### Developers

GitHub matters most here. Your handle shows up on every commit, every pull request, every package you publish. It's essentially your professional signature. The npm registry, PyPI, crates.io, and personal domains all benefit from matching it.

Good patterns: `alexcodes`, `devjsmith`, `stacksarah`, `buildwithjake`, `codebymike`, `jakedev`, `sarahjs`, `mikepy`, `alexrs` (language suffix for niche specialization).

The [GitHub](/check/github) + npm + personal domain trifecta is what you're really optimizing for. If you can get `alexcodes` on GitHub and register `alexcodes.dev`, you're set. People will find your open source work, your blog, and your packages under one name. That discoverability compounds over time, especially as you contribute to more projects.

### Designers

Portfolio platforms are the priority: [Behance](/check/behance), [Dribbble](/check/dribbble), personal site. Your username shows up in portfolio URLs that end up in job applications and client proposals.

Good patterns: `sarahdraws`, `designbymike`, `pixelalex`, `studiojake`, `craftedbyjen`, `jenmakes`, `alexvisual`, `sarahux`, `mikeui`.

The `studioname` format works especially well for designers who freelance under a brand rather than their personal name. If you already have a studio name, use that everywhere instead.

### Writers and journalists

Bylines matter. Editors and readers search your name. Your [Medium](/check/medium) handle, [Substack](/check/substack) subdomain, and Twitter/X presence should all point to the same person.

Good patterns: `tomwrites`, `wordsbysarah`, `editormike`, `prosealex`, `sarahink`, `jakewords`, `mikebyline`, `alexedits`.

For journalists specifically, `firstname.lastname` or `firstnamelastname` is the strongest move. You want editors to find you, and they'll search your actual name. A clever handle can actually hurt you here by making you harder to look up.

### Consultants and freelancers

Your username doubles as a brand. It shows up in proposals, invoices, and client-facing communications. Professionalism matters more than personality in this context.

Good patterns: `hirejakem`, `sarahconsults`, `advisoralex`, `strategybymike`, `jenconsulting`, `alexadvisory`, `mikefreelance`, `tomstrategy`.

The `hirename` pattern is bold but effective. It's a call to action baked into a username. Works best on platforms where potential clients actually browse profiles, like LinkedIn or [Twitter](/check/twitter).

### Creators and influencers

Even creators who lean casual on [Instagram](/check/instagram) or [TikTok](/check/tiktok) benefit from a clean, real-name-adjacent handle on professional platforms. The brand handle goes on content platforms; the professional handle goes on LinkedIn and GitHub.

Good patterns: `heysarah`, `imjake`, `thealexshow`, `realmikeco`, `jenofficial`, `bytom`, `helloalex`, `itsmikej`.

If you're a creator who also does business development, consulting, or speaking, consider maintaining two identities: your creator brand and your legal name. LinkedIn should always be your real name, period.

## The consistency trifecta

A professional username only works if it's the same everywhere that matters. The places where your career lives online should all point to one identity:

- **LinkedIn URL:** `linkedin.com/in/yourname`
- **GitHub handle:** `github.com/yourname`
- **Personal domain:** `yourname.dev` or `yourname.com`
- **Email:** `hello@yourname.com` or `yourname@gmail.com`

When all four match, anyone who knows one of them can guess the others. A recruiter who finds your GitHub can find your LinkedIn. A client who visits your website can find your email. There's no guessing, no confusion, no dead ends.

This matters more than most people think. Recruiters in tech often cross-reference GitHub and LinkedIn profiles. If your [GitHub handle doesn't match your LinkedIn](/blog/same-username-everywhere), they might not connect the two, and you lose credit for your open source work. Hiring managers google candidates. If your name returns a clean, consistent set of professional profiles, that's a strong signal. Fragmented handles suggest someone who doesn't pay attention to details.

## What not to do

A few patterns that hurt you on professional platforms:

Trailing numbers are the worst offender. `mikesmith47` or `sarah_j_2024` screams "my first choice was taken and I gave up." It's fine on a gaming platform. It undermines credibility on LinkedIn. Year-based suffixes are especially bad because they'll look dated within 12 months.

Trendy prefixes don't belong here either. `xo.sarah` or `vibesbymike` are content platform names, not professional ones. Trends expire. Your career doesn't. If you'd hesitate to put it on a business card, don't put it on LinkedIn.

Don't use pseudonyms on LinkedIn. They enforce real-name policies, and they do act on reports. Using a fake name or a brand name as your personal profile name risks having your account restricted. If you want a brand presence on LinkedIn, create a company page. Understanding the [difference between usernames and display names](/blog/username-vs-display-name) helps here.

Keep it under 15 characters. Anything longer is hard to type, hard to remember, and gets truncated in some contexts. If your full name is already 20 characters, you need to abbreviate. Check the [character limits for each platform](/blog/username-character-limits) to know exactly where you'll run into problems.

Inconsistency is the other big mistake. Being `msmith` on GitHub but `michael.smith.consulting` on LinkedIn and `smithm` in your email is actively working against you. Pick one pattern and [use it everywhere](/blog/same-username-everywhere).
